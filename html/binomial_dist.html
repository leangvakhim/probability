Step 1

The Bernoulli Trial

Before we understand the Binomial Distribution, we must start with a single event. This is called a Bernoulli trial—an experiment with exactly two possible outcomes: "Success" or "Failure".

Real-World Example

Imagine a basketball player taking a single free throw. They either make the shot (Success) or miss it (Failure).

Let's say our player has a 70% chance of making a shot ($p = 0.7$). Therefore, the chance of missing ($1 - p$) is 30%.

Step 2

Multiple Independent Trials ($n$)

The Binomial Distribution looks at what happens when we repeat this single trial multiple times. A crucial rule is that these trials must be independent, meaning the outcome of one doesn't affect the next.

Real-World Example

Our player now takes 3 free throws in a row ($n = 3$). Each individual shot still has that same 70% chance of going in. We want to find the probabilities of all the possible overall outcomes (making 0, 1, 2, or 3 shots).

Step 3

Targeting Successes ($k$)

We often want to find the probability of getting exactly $k$ successes out of our $n$ trials. To calculate this correctly, we must account for all the different order combinations those successes can happen.

Real-World Example

What is the probability of making exactly 2 out of 3 shots ($k = 2$)?

It could happen 3 different ways. Since each way has the same probability ($0.7 \times 0.7 \times 0.3 = 0.147$), we add them up (or multiply by 3). The total probability is 44.1%.

Final Step

The Binomial Formula

Putting it all together, we can summarize everything into one powerful equation. It calculates the probability $P(X=k)$ of getting exactly $k$ successes in $n$ independent trials.

$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} \cdot p^k \cdot (1-p)^{n-k}$$
$$\binom{n}{k}$$

Combinations

The number of different ways we can pick exactly $k$ successes out of $n$ trials.

$\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$
$$p^k$$

Successes

The probability of the success ($p$) happening exactly $k$ times.

$$(1-p)^{n-k}$$

Failures

The probability of failure ($1-p$) happening the remaining number of times ($n-k$).
